# Spec Wars: the engineer vs. contractor RPG bible

**Federal construction projects are bureaucratic warzones where engineers and contractors fight with paper, not fists — and the weapons are specification clauses, RFIs, and exquisitely passive-aggressive emails.** The adversarial dynamics on NAVFAC and USACE projects are rich enough to fuel an entire RPG franchise. Below is a comprehensive compendium of authentic tactics, real dialogue, character archetypes, and combat scenarios drawn from federal construction culture — organized for direct use in a turn-based RPG where an engineer and contractor battle each other using the actual tools of construction warfare.

---

## The combatants and their class systems

The two sides of this war operate under rigid hierarchies, and understanding the chain of command is essential for authentic RPG character design.

**The Government/Engineer Party:**
The **Contracting Officer (CO/KO)** is the final boss — the *only* person who can legally bind the government. Below them sits the **Contracting Officer's Representative (COR)**, who has delegated authority but **cannot change the contract** — a mid-level boss who talks big but can't deliver. The **Quality Assurance Representative** is the field inspector who can reject work but can't approve changes. The **Project Engineer** reviews technical submittals and recommends decisions. And the **Safety Officer** outranks everyone in practice — they can shut down a $50 million project because someone's hard hat sticker is expired.

**The Contractor Party:**
The **Project Manager** is the strategist who manages budget, schedule, and the inevitable claim. The **Superintendent** is the field general — 30 years of experience, hates paperwork, calls every engineer "kid" regardless of age. The **QC Manager** is perpetually caught between what the super wants to do and what the spec requires. And when things go sideways, in walks the **Claims Consultant** — arriving like a vulture circling a dying animal, capable of finding a "constructive change" in a weather report.

### Character stereotypes that ring painfully true

The **Green Government Inspector** quotes specification sections by number but has never seen the materials actually installed. Rejects work for cosmetic issues while missing structural problems. Strong opinions about concrete finish despite never having mixed a batch. Opening line: *"According to the Unified Facilities Guide Specification…"*

The **Grizzled Superintendent** treats specifications as "suggestions" and drawings as "starting points." Can estimate a concrete pour by eye within two cubic yards. His battle cry is **"That's how we've always done it"** and he hates paperwork more than rain delays. Refers to the engineer as "the kid."

The **Claims Consultant** writes 200-page entitlement analyses for $3,000 change orders. Speaks exclusively in "quantum" and "entitlement" and "impact analysis." Makes government blood pressure spike just by showing up at a meeting. *"Have you been keeping daily logs? Good. They're evidence now."*

The **Spec Lawyer** reads every comma like it's load-bearing. Can argue for 45 minutes about whether "or equal" means "equivalent" or "identical." Their version of small talk is debating whether semicolons create independent clauses in contract language. *"The specification says 'shall,' not 'should' — there's a difference."*

## The contractor's offensive arsenal

Contractors on federal projects have developed an entire toolkit of bureaucratic combat moves, each with real-world mechanics that translate perfectly into RPG attacks.

### RFI Bombardment (the contractor's primary weapon)

The Request for Information is nominally a clarification tool. In practice, it is the contractor's **most versatile weapon**. Engineers do NOT submit RFIs — they respond to them. The Navigant Construction Forum's landmark study of 1,362 projects with over one million RFIs found the average RFI costs **$1,080 to process** and takes approximately eight hours of review time. An average project generates **796 RFIs**, costing roughly **$860,000** in review effort alone. Approximately **13.2%** are unjustifiable — the answer already exists in the contract documents.

**Five RFI attack modes for the RPG:**

- **Paper Trail Bombing** — Flood the engineer with hundreds of RFIs to establish a paper trail for future "faulty design" claims. The courtroom pitch: *"There were 4,000 RFIs on this project — clearly the design was incomplete."* In one real case involving a wastewater treatment facility, a contractor claimed ~4,000 RFIs. Forensic analysis revealed only ~350 were genuine — the rest were submittals disguised as RFIs, repackaged correspondence, fishing expeditions, and the same question resubmitted with different wording.

- **Response-Time Trapping** — Overwhelm the engineer with RFIs hoping they miss contractual response deadlines. The contractor then claims: *"We couldn't proceed because the engineer didn't answer our RFI."* RPG effect: forces the engineer to either rush a response (risking errors) or miss the deadline (feeding the delay claim).

- **Ambiguity Mining** — Submit RFIs specifically targeting spec ambiguities that were identifiable before bidding but deliberately ignored to maintain a low bid price. Post-award, these become leverage. Classic RFI language: *"The specifications appear to conflict between Section XX and Drawing YY…"*

- **Design Decision Forcing** — Word the RFI so the engineer must choose between options, then argue the response constituted a constructive change. *"Please advise whether the contractor should proceed with Option A or Option B…"* If the engineer picks the expensive option, change order. If the engineer picks the cheap option, the contractor was going to do that anyway.

- **Schedule Hostage** — Submit an RFI on an activity with less than 10 working days of float, then claim delay when the standard review period consumes the remaining float. The magic phrase: *"Please be advised that failure to respond to this RFI within [X] days will impact the critical path."*

### Submittal sleight-of-hand

**The "Or Equal" Gambit:** Contractor bids on the specified expensive product, wins the project, then immediately submits an "or equal" for a cheaper product and pockets the difference. One industry insider admitted: *"Those loopholes and post-award substitution acceptance were what made me money on the contracting side."* As federal courts have noted: *"If the Government contracts for a Cadillac, the Government is entitled to a Cadillac — any vehicle that runs may be 'acceptable,' but that does not make every operating vehicle equal to a Cadillac."*

**The Substitution Smuggle:** Slip substitution requests into the regular submittal process rather than using the formal substitution procedure. The construction equivalent of hoping your partner won't notice you bought generic instead of name brand. They always notice.

**The Resubmittal Loop:** Submit a non-spec product, get rejected, resubmit with minor cosmetic changes to the documentation (not the product), hoping a different reviewer processes it or the original reviewer caves from fatigue. Each cycle burns 2–4 weeks of the engineer's review time.

**The Incomplete Package:** Submit partial information making proper evaluation impossible, triggering "Revise and Resubmit" — which the contractor later cites as government-caused delay.

### Claims and contract clause warfare

**Differing Site Conditions (FAR 52.236-2)** is the single most powerful weapon in the federal contractor's arsenal. It shifts the risk of unforeseen subsurface conditions from contractor to government. **Type I claims** argue conditions differ materially from those indicated in contract documents. **Type II claims** argue conditions are unusual and different from what's ordinarily encountered. The smart contractor stops work immediately, photographs everything, and fires off the magic letter: *"Pursuant to FAR 52.236-2, we are providing prompt written notice of a potentially differing site condition encountered on [date] at [location]…"* In one real case, a contractor claimed differing site conditions when they hit rock — while rock was visibly protruding from the surface during the pre-bid site visit.

**The Constructive Change Claim** is triggered when the government directs work beyond what the contractor believes the contract requires. Under the Spearin Doctrine, when the government provides the design, it impliedly warrants its adequacy. In *Metric Construction v. United States*, the Corps' unclear RFI response caused damages — the court ruled it was a constructive change and awarded **$1.3 million**.

**Value Engineering Change Proposals (VECPs)** under FAR 52.248-3 let contractors propose cheaper alternatives and keep **55% of net savings**. The game: identify the cheaper alternative during estimating, bid the expensive specified item, win the contract, then submit a VECP to switch and pocket the savings split. USACE acceptance rate for VECPs has historically **exceeded 60%**.

**The "Perform Under Protest" Play:** When ordered to do disputed work, the contractor sends a letter stating: *"This work is being performed under protest and with full reservation of right to seek an increase in the contract price and additional time."* This preserves the claim while keeping the project moving.

### Schedule manipulation and float wars

**"Who owns the float?"** is one of the most contentious questions in federal construction. Contractors plan for early completion, then when the government causes any delay that consumes float, claim a "frustrated early completion" for unabsorbed overhead. **CPM schedule gaming** involves manipulating logical ties, lead/lag times, and constraints to show government activities as the sole critical path driver. The classic schedule narrative evolution: Month 1: "We're on schedule!" Month 3: "Minor weather delay." Month 6: Schedule update shows 47 RFIs on the critical path — all the government's fault. Month 9: The schedule narrative reads like a legal brief.

## The engineer's defensive and counter-attack toolkit

Government engineers fight with specification precision, bureaucratic authority, and the immense leverage of holding the checkbook.

### Specification language as spell-casting

The word **"SHALL"** is the engineer's most powerful incantation. In federal specs, "shall" is a mandatory, non-negotiable, legally binding obligation. Engineers weaponize it constantly: *"The specification states the contractor SHALL — this is not optional."* The hierarchy: **"SHALL"** = mandatory (contractor's duty); **"WILL"** = government's future action; **"SHOULD"** = recommended but not required; **"MAY"** = truly optional. Engineers exploit the moment a contractor treats "shall" as if it were "may."

The standard citation attack: *"Per Section 07 92 00, paragraph 3.2.1, the contractor SHALL apply sealant in strict accordance with manufacturer's written instructions. 'Shall' is mandatory, not discretionary."* UFC and UFGS specifications are **mandatory for all DoD construction** — citing them is the engineer's equivalent of invoking divine authority.

### Submittal review as damage-dealing

USACE uses specific action codes on submittals, each with escalating consequences:

| Code | Meaning | RPG Effect |
|------|---------|------------|
| **A** | Approved as submitted | Contractor heals (rare drop) |
| **B** | Approved except as noted; no resubmission | Minor contractor buff |
| **C** | Approved except as noted; resubmission required | 2-4 week delay inflicted |
| **D** | Returned by separate correspondence | Bureaucratic purgatory |
| **E** | Disapproved | Nuclear option; full redo |

The critical fine print on every approval: *"Approval does not relieve the Contractor from complying with all contract requirements; will not prevent the Contracting Officer from requiring removal and replacement of nonconforming material."* Translation: even a Code A doesn't protect you.

### The three-phase inspection system (a three-hit combo)

Every Definable Feature of Work requires **Preparatory** (pre-work review and personnel verification), **Initial** (first article inspection — if it doesn't pass, ALL work stops), and **Follow-up** (daily monitoring generating NCRs). This creates three separate opportunities to catch and document contractor failures. The first article inspection is particularly devastating: one failed pour and the entire concrete program halts.

### Bureaucratic escalation chain (the engineer's ultimate abilities)

**Non-Conformance Report (NCR):** Documents deviation permanently. Becomes part of the project record and contractor performance evaluations. Items get physically marked on-site for maximum visibility.

**Cure Notice (FAR 49.402-3):** Written notice with minimum **10-day countdown** to fix a failure — or face termination. The engineer's equivalent of lighting a fuse.

**Show Cause Letter:** Final warning. *"Show cause why the contract should not be terminated for default."* The response: *"Failure to present an explanation may be taken as an admission that no valid explanation exists."*

**Stop Work Order:** Halts all work for up to **90 days**. Requires authority above the contracting officer — this is the engineer calling in an airstrike.

**Default Termination (the nuclear option):** Ends the contract, makes the contractor liable for excess reprocurement costs, and gets reported to federal databases — devastating future bidding capability for years. The safety valve: if the termination was improper, it converts to a "Termination for Convenience" — which is actually favorable to the contractor.

**Retainage** is a passive ability always in effect: **5-10%** of every payment is held until final acceptance of ALL work. Combined with **liquidated damages** — a pre-set daily dollar penalty for every day past the deadline — the government's financial leverage is enormous.

## Authentic dialogue for RPG combat encounters

The best RPG dialogue comes straight from real construction correspondence. Every phrase below has been used on actual federal projects, and each one barely conceals the hostility underneath.

### Engineer attack lines

*"Per the contract documents…"* — The opening salvo. Translation: "I'm about to cite the exact clause that proves you're wrong."

*"As noted in our previous correspondence…"* — Translation: "I already told you this. Can you read?"

*"This does not conform to the approved submittal."* — A critical hit.

*"Remove and replace at no additional cost to the Government."* — Maximum damage.

*"The specification is clear and unambiguous."* — Blocking the contractor's ambiguity claim.

*"The contractor bid this work. The time for questioning the specifications was during the bidding period."* — Shutting the door on post-award complaints.

*"Your QC system has failed to prevent this deficiency."* — Using the contractor's own QC plan as a weapon.

*"The Government is concerned with the end result, not how you get there. If the end result doesn't meet specifications, remove and replace at your expense."* — The "means and methods" counter-attack.

*"Approval of submittals does not relieve the contractor from complying with all contract requirements."* — The fine print that destroys contractor arguments.

*"We look forward to the Contractor's corrective action plan."* — Said with the energy of a shark smiling at a swimmer.

*"Please be advised…"* — Translation: "I'm about to ruin your day, formally."

*"Your attention to this matter is appreciated."* — Translation: "Do it now."

### Contractor attack lines

*"We reserve our rights under the contract."* — The defensive shield. Translation: "We're filing a claim later."

*"We consider this direction to be a constructive change to our contract."* — Activating the claim.

*"The specifications are ambiguous regarding…"* — Exposing a gap in the engineer's armor.

*"This is not within our scope of work as defined by the contract documents."* — Scope creep defense.

*"This is a means and methods issue — the contractor controls means and methods."* — Deflecting government interference.

*"Please confirm this direction in writing from the Contracting Officer."* — Calling out when a COR oversteps authority.

*"We cannot determine at this time the effect on the contract completion date…"* — Translation: "Oh, we can determine it. We're just not telling you yet."

*"The Contractor hereby provides notice of a potential impact to the contract price and/or schedule…"* — The claim setup letter has been cast.

*"We assure you that we will do everything we can to minimize those costs…"* — Translation: "We have done absolutely nothing to minimize those costs."

*"I've been doing this for 30 years and I've never had to do it that way."* — The superintendent's signature move.

### Meeting combat dialogue

**Pre-battle formalities:**
*"Before we begin, let me note that this meeting does not constitute a waiver of any rights under the contract."* — Both sides actually say this.

*"We're here today in a spirit of partnering and collaboration."* — Said immediately before a two-hour screaming match.

**Mid-battle exchanges:**

Engineer: *"The work does not conform to the contract requirements."*
Contractor: *"Which version of the drawings? We've received four sets of revisions."*

Contractor: *"This is a constructive change."*
Engineer: *"This is contract compliance."*

Contractor: *"We need more time."*
Engineer: *"The completion date is the point at which liquidated damages begin."*

Superintendent: *"We're not building a piano."*
Inspector: *"No, you're building a military facility to UFC standards."*

Contractor: *"That's how we've always done it."*
Engineer: *"That's not what the spec says."*
Contractor: *"The spec was written by someone who's never built anything."*
Engineer: *"And yet here we are, contractually bound by it."*

Contractor: *"Where do you want this wall?"*
Engineer: *"Where the drawing says."*
Contractor: *"The drawing shows it going through an existing utility."*
Engineer: *"…Submit an RFI."*

**Post-battle declarations:**
*"We will memorialize this discussion in the meeting minutes."* — Translation: "Everything you just said is now evidence."

*"This matter is referred to the Contracting Officer for a final decision."* — Escalation to the final boss.

*"We'll take this under advisement."* — Translation: "This goes in the drawer and dies."

## Ten RPG battle scenarios ripped from real projects

**Scenario 1 — "The Phantom Submittal"**
Contractor installs material before the submittal is approved. Government arrives on-site. *"Was this submittal approved?"* Contractor: *"We submitted it."* Engineer: *"Submitting does not equal approved. The stamp says 'Revise and Resubmit.'"* Contractor: *"But the lead time was 12 weeks!"* Engineer: *"That's a 'you' problem. Remove and replace."*

**Scenario 2 — "The RFI Flood"**
Contractor submits 47 RFIs in one week, each one targeting a different ambiguity. Engineer recognizes the claim setup. Counter-move: respond to all 47 with *"The information requested is contained in the contract documents. The Contractor is responsible for reviewing all contract documents prior to bidding."*

**Scenario 3 — "The Or-Equal Duel"**
Spec listed products at $800 each. Contractor submits cut sheets for a $200 product. When rejected, contractor argues: *"This is what we always use,"* then *"Other engineers let us substitute this,"* then *"The base approved this for Building 101."* The one argument NOT made: that the product is actually equal. Engineer's finishing move: *"If the Government contracts for a Cadillac, the Government is entitled to a Cadillac."*

**Scenario 4 — "The Differing Site Conditions Gambit"**
Contractor hits rock during excavation. Files DSC claim under FAR 52.236-2. Engineer pulls up the pre-bid site visit photos showing rock visibly protruding from the surface. *"The boring logs were included in the contract documents. The contractor was obligated to review all available information."* Contractor's last resort: *"The Government's mere silence is insufficient to establish the absence of unfavorable conditions."*

**Scenario 5 — "Value Engineering or Just Cutting Corners?"**
Contractor proposes replacing specified material with a cheaper alternative, framed as a VECP with a projected savings split of 55/45. Engineer examines the 10-year lifecycle cost nobody calculated and responds: *"That's a nice Value Engineering proposal. Now show me where it meets the performance spec."* Contractor: *"It meets the MINIMUM requirements."* Engineer: *"The Government did not specify the minimum. The Government specified the standard."*

**Scenario 6 — "Death by Three-Phase Inspection"**
The first article concrete pour fails the initial phase inspection. QA rep: *"The slump test is out of range. The placement doesn't match the approved mix design."* Superintendent: *"It's close enough."* QA: *"Close enough isn't in the UFGS."* All concrete operations halt until the contractor demonstrates compliance. Days pass. Liquidated damages accumulate.

**Scenario 7 — "The COR Trap"**
Contractor asks the COR if fire-rated plywood is needed everywhere. COR says no. Contractor proceeds. The Contracting Officer later says yes. Contractor has to rip out and replace. Contractor cries constructive change. Government's defense: *"Only the Contracting Officer can bind the Government. The COR does not have authority to modify the contract."* Contractor learns the hard way about the authority hierarchy.

**Scenario 8 — "Schedule Jiu-Jitsu"**
Contractor's Month 6 schedule update suddenly shows 47 government-caused delays on the critical path. Engineer examines the CPM logic and finds manipulated lag times, artificial constraints, and float sequestration. Counter-attack: *"Your schedule update does not reflect actual logic. The float manipulation in Activities 340 through 380 is inconsistent with your approved baseline. Revise and resubmit per UFGS 01 32 01."* Contractor: *"We reserve our rights."*

**Scenario 9 — "The Pre-Final Inspection from Hell"**
Contractor declares substantial completion. Government arrives with eight inspectors, flashlights, moisture meters, and decades of accumulated grievances. The resulting punch list runs to 47 pages. Retainage — the **5-10% of ALL payments** the government has been holding — stays frozen until every item is closed, including the ceiling tile installed 0.5mm off-center. Contractor's QC manager begins questioning his career choices.

**Scenario 10 — "The Partnering Session Betrayal"**
Both parties attend the preconstruction partnering session. Everyone pledges to "communicate openly" and "resolve issues at the lowest level." They sign a partnering charter. By the second progress meeting, every email is cc'd to supervisors three levels up. The partnering charter now hangs on the trailer wall, yellowing — a relic of lost innocence.

## Conclusion: why this RPG writes itself

The federal construction battlefield offers something rare in game design — **a conflict system where both sides have deeply asymmetric but balanced arsenals, escalating stakes, and dialogue that is simultaneously formal and venomous.** The contractor fights with RFIs, claims, schedule manipulation, and the inexorable leverage of being the only one who can actually build anything. The engineer fights with specification precision, quality inspection systems, payment withholding, and the ultimate threat of termination. Both sides fight with the paper trail — because in federal construction, if it isn't documented, it didn't happen, and if it IS documented, it's evidence.

The deepest humor comes from the gap between the formal language and the real meaning. Every *"we respectfully disagree"* is a declaration of war. Every *"as noted in our previous correspondence"* is an accusation of illiteracy. Every partnering charter is a treaty waiting to be broken. The authentic comedy of federal construction isn't about incompetence — it's about two highly competent adversaries locked in bureaucratic combat, each wielding the same contract against the other, each convinced the other is acting in bad faith, and each absolutely correct about that.
