# SPEC BATTLE — NAVFAC/FAR Adversarial Dialog Source Material

A reference for authoring in-character dialog between a NAVFAC Resident Officer in Charge of Construction (ROICC) / Government engineer and a commercial construction contractor's project manager. Organized by the twelve game moves. Cites real FAR clauses, UFC volumes, UFGS sections, and editorial rules from UFS 1-300-02. Humor lives in the friction between what the documents say and how humans actually fight over them.

---

## ENGINEER MOVES

### REJECT SUBMITTAL

**What it is.** The Government reviewer returns a contractor submittal (shop drawings, product data, samples, Quality Control plan, schedule, test reports) with an action code indicating it cannot be used. Under the NAVFAC submittal register, the action codes on ENG Form 4025 / NAVFAC 4330/43 run A (Approved) / B (Approved as Noted) / C (Revise and Resubmit) / D (Disapproved) / E (Not Reviewed / Receipt Acknowledged). Anything C or worse means the Contractor does not have government acceptance and cannot proceed with fabrication, procurement, or installation of that item.

**Canonical references.**
- UFGS 01 33 00 *Submittal Procedures* — submittal classifications (SD-01 through SD-11), Government Approved (G) vs. Information Only (FIO) designators, reviewer action definitions.
- FAR 52.246-12 *Inspection of Construction* — Government's right to inspect and reject.
- FAR 52.236-5 *Material and Workmanship* — basis for conformity demand.
- NAVFAC Red Zone / three-phase inspection concept (Preparatory, Initial, Follow-up) per UFGS 01 45 00.00 20 *Quality Control*.
- The contract's Submittal Register — the enumerated, contract-binding list of what has to be submitted, in what SD category, with what flag.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "Returned — Code C. Revise and resubmit."
- "Non-conforming as submitted. See comments."
- "You listed this as SD-03 Product Data. It's an SD-02 Shop Drawing. Re-bundle it."
- "Approval is not implied by review. You're still on the hook for the contract requirements."
- "This is the third iteration. I'm not paying for my reviewer's time forever — the delay is concurrent and compensable to the Government, not you."
- "G-stamped items are not 'acceptance' — they authorize fabrication only."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs SUBMIT RFI.** The RFI is the contractor's attempt to force the spec to interpret itself. Engineer's counter: if the submittal is already non-conforming on its face, the RFI is premature and does not toll the submittal clock. "The RFI doesn't save a bad submittal — revise, then ask."
- **vs CLAIM DSC.** DSC is a site condition defense; it doesn't fix a paperwork problem. "The soil report has nothing to do with the fact your Q-list is missing three required qualifications."
- **vs VALUE ENGINEER.** A VECP can't arrive through the submittal channel wearing a product-data disguise. "If you want to change the spec, file a VECP under FAR 52.248-3. Don't smuggle it into the shop drawings."
- **vs SCHEDULE DELAY.** Submittal review time is baked into the CPM; missed review windows are usually the contractor's scheduling problem. "You submitted SD-02 for the AHU on 14 March and need approval by 04 April. Review is 20 calendar days under 01 33 00. Do the arithmetic before you blame me."
- **vs OR-EQUAL GAMBIT.** "Or equal" does not mean "approved by submittal." The burden of proof is on the contractor per FAR 52.236-5 and the salient characteristics in Section 01 60 00. "Not approved. Your product data doesn't address half the salient characteristics I listed."
- **vs RESERVE RIGHTS.** "Reserving rights" does not stay my reject. "Preserve whatever you want — the submittal is still Code C until it conforms."

### RED-LINE SPEC

**What it is.** Line-by-line annotation on a contractor submittal, design deliverable, or RFI response that marks deficiencies in red ink (or red strikeout in Bluebeam / Adobe). In design-build contracts under UFC 1-300-09N, the Government reviews the DOR's (Designer of Record's) drawings/specs and red-lines departures from the RFP technical requirements. In design-bid-build, the ROICC red-lines shop drawings against the contract specs.

**Canonical references.**
- UFGS 01 33 00 — reviewer markup conventions.
- UFC 1-300-09N *Design Procedures* — design-build review process and red-line cycles.
- UFS 1-300-02 — editorial rules that the engineer weaponizes when the contractor's submittal imports unenforceable language.
- NAVFAC Basis of Design (BOD) and 35% / 65% / 95% design reviews — where red-line combat peaks.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "Every highlighted line is a deficiency. Count them. Now count them again."
- "Your spec section copy-pasted 'in a neat and workmanlike manner' — that's a §2-4.4 violation, it's unenforceable, I red-lined it out."
- "This isn't a suggestion. The red ink is the revised text. Incorporate verbatim."
- "Submittal's been in my red pen longer than it was in your CAD system."
- "I red-lined your red-lines. Your turn."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs SUBMIT RFI.** Red-lines pre-empt the RFI by giving the answer. "If you'd read the markup you wouldn't need to ask. See sheet 3 of my comments."
- **vs CLAIM DSC.** DSC is irrelevant to what's on paper. Unless the red-line demands work that the site conditions prevent, it stands.
- **vs VALUE ENGINEER.** A red-line that narrows a requirement may foreclose a VECP's engineering space. Engineer can point at the red-lined salient characteristics and say "your VE doesn't meet the red-lined performance. Dead on arrival."
- **vs SCHEDULE DELAY.** Contractor tries to claim the red-line cycle itself causes delay. Engineer's counter: 01 33 00 allotted review time — the contractor's repeated failures to incorporate comments are the float-burner.
- **vs OR-EQUAL GAMBIT.** Red-lining the salient characteristics list preempts "or equal" substitutions by tightening what "equal" means. "I red-lined six characteristics into the table. Your 'equal' doesn't satisfy four of them."
- **vs RESERVE RIGHTS.** "Your reservation is noted in the margin. Next to my red-line. Which still governs."

### INVOKE SHALL

**What it is.** The mandatory-obligation verb. In legacy federal specifications "shall" meant "the Contractor is obligated to" — which made every "shall" clause a potential default trigger. In modern UFGS written to UFS 1-300-02 (effective update cycle), **the word "shall" is prohibited in specifications**: §2-4.4 requires imperative mood ("Install equipment.") for actions directed to the Contractor and "must" for third-party subjects. The Engineer character is a 25-year-veteran who learned the craft when "SHALL" was sacred, knows UFS banned it, and keeps using it anyway because the legacy contract documents still contain tens of thousands of "shalls" that remain enforceable.

**Canonical references.**
- UFS 1-300-02 §2-4.4 *Prohibited Words and Phrases* — the ban itself. "The use of 'shall' is prohibited."
- Legacy UFGS sections dated before the editorial cycle that adopted §2-4.4 — still binding on awarded contracts.
- FAR 52.236-5 *Material and Workmanship* — "shall" language.
- FAR 52.246-12 *Inspection of Construction* — legacy "shall" usage.
- Court and board case law (ASBCA) interpreting "shall" as mandatory, not directory.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "The spec says SHALL. Not should, not may, not if-you-feel-like-it. SHALL."
- "I know §2-4.4 prohibits it. I don't care. The contract we awarded still has 3,000 'shalls' in it and every one of them is enforceable."
- "UFS can ban the word all it likes in future solicitations. Your contract was signed in 2022. Read clause 3.7."
- "They took the word out of the rulebook and my discipline out with it. You want me to say 'Install ductwork'? That sounds like a recipe, not a contract."
- "'Shall' means obligation. Always has. If you wanted 'should' you should have bid a different job."
- "Yes, I'm invoking a prohibited word. I'm also invoking the $4 million bond that says you shall — there, again — perform."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs SUBMIT RFI.** RFI asking what "shall" means. "Shall = mandatory. Asked and answered fifty years of case law ago. That RFI costs you a day."
- **vs CLAIM DSC.** "The soil didn't change the verb. You shall grout the bolts regardless of what Type II condition you think you found."
- **vs VALUE ENGINEER.** VECP tries to relax a "shall" into a "may." "You're proposing to convert mandatory language to discretionary. That's not value engineering, that's a scope reduction and the Government keeps 100% of it."
- **vs SCHEDULE DELAY.** "Schedule doesn't overrule the word. You shall finish by the revised contract completion date."
- **vs OR-EQUAL GAMBIT.** "The spec says the pump shall deliver 450 GPM at 60 feet TDH. Your 'or equal' delivers 430. The verb doesn't flex."
- **vs RESERVE RIGHTS.** "Reserve all you want. You shall still perform in the meantime."

### ISSUE NCR

**What it is.** Non-Conformance Report. Formal written documentation — typically on a NAVFAC or Government-approved QC form — that in-place work or delivered materials deviate from the contract. Triggers a required root-cause analysis and corrective action from the Contractor QC organization. Tracked in the QC daily report, flagged at preparatory and initial phase meetings, and escalates to Government deficiency reports if not corrected. Under the three-phase control system, NCRs typically surface during Follow-up Phase inspections.

**Canonical references.**
- UFGS 01 45 00.00 20 *Quality Control* — three-phase inspection (Preparatory / Initial / Follow-up), QC daily report requirements, non-conformance tracking.
- USACE EP 715-1-2 / NAVFAC equivalent — Contractor Quality Management concepts.
- FAR 52.246-12 — Government inspection authority and rejection of non-conforming work.
- UFC 1-300-08 *Criteria for Transfer and Acceptance of DoD Real Property* — Beneficial Occupancy Date predicate conditions.
- Contract-specific QC Plan approved under Section 01 45 00.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "NCR-037. Rebar cover fails 1/2-inch minimum per ACI 318. Rip it out."
- "This is going in the QC daily and on the Government deficiency log."
- "You want to close this NCR, I need root cause, corrective action, and preventive action — not a promise to do better."
- "Preparatory phase was a week ago. You told me you had a Crew B. This isn't Crew B's work."
- "Three open NCRs on this CLIN. I'm not signing pay app 6 until at least two close."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs SUBMIT RFI.** "The RFI doesn't close the NCR. The NCR is about what you built, not what you plan to build. Different clock."
- **vs CLAIM DSC.** Rare overlap — if the DSC truly caused the non-conformance (e.g., unexpected rock forced shallower footing), the NCR may be superseded by a modification. Engineer's posture: "Show me the contracting officer's determination on the DSC before I touch this NCR."
- **vs VALUE ENGINEER.** "You can't VECP your way out of defective work. VE is a proposal to change the spec for future work; this is already installed and wrong."
- **vs SCHEDULE DELAY.** "Your rework is not an excusable delay. That's non-compensable and not a time extension."
- **vs OR-EQUAL GAMBIT.** NCR on a product installed without prior "or equal" approval. "You installed a substitute without a submittal. That's two findings: unapproved substitution AND non-conformance. Remove and replace."
- **vs RESERVE RIGHTS.** "Reserving rights doesn't toll the corrective-action clock. You have 14 days to submit CAPA or I escalate."

### CITE UFC

**What it is.** Referencing a Unified Facilities Criteria document — the DoD-wide mandatory technical criteria published on the Whole Building Design Guide (WBDG) by a rotating tri-service executive agent. UFCs are incorporated by reference in virtually every NAVFAC contract via Section 01 33 00 or the RFP technical requirements. Citing a UFC is often how the Engineer wins a technical argument when the UFGS section is silent or general — the UFC provides the "shall" (or post-§2-4.4, the "must") the contractor is trying to avoid.

**Canonical references (sample — there are hundreds).**
- UFC 1-200-01 *DoD Building Code* — baseline building code applicability.
- UFC 1-200-02 *High Performance and Sustainable Building Requirements*.
- UFC 3-301-01 *Structural Engineering*.
- UFC 3-310-04 *Seismic Design for Buildings*.
- UFC 3-401-01 *Mechanical Engineering*.
- UFC 3-501-01 *Electrical Engineering*.
- UFC 3-600-01 *Fire Protection Engineering for Facilities*.
- UFC 4-010-01 *DoD Minimum Antiterrorism Standards for Buildings*.
- UFC 1-300-09N *Design Procedures* — the NAVFAC procedural bible.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "UFC 3-600-01, Table 3-1. Standpipes required. I don't care what the local AHJ told you at the pre-bid."
- "Read UFC 4-010-01 §1-3. Standoff distance is mandatory, not advisory."
- "Your DOR missed UFC 3-310-04 §2-2.1. That's why the shear wall reinforcement is light."
- "The criteria are incorporated by reference. I don't need to paste them into the spec."
- "UFC governs. IBC is a floor, not a ceiling."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs SUBMIT RFI.** "The UFC answers your RFI. Paragraph 3-4.2. Read it before you ask again."
- **vs CLAIM DSC.** UFC on geotechnical (UFC 3-220-01) defines the expected baseline; if the actual conditions fall inside the UFC envelope, no DSC. "UFC 3-220-01 contemplates this soil profile. Not differing. Not compensable."
- **vs VALUE ENGINEER.** "The UFC sets the floor. Your VE proposes to go below it. Rejected."
- **vs SCHEDULE DELAY.** "UFC doesn't change on your schedule. Non-starter."
- **vs OR-EQUAL GAMBIT.** "UFC 3-401-01 names the specific performance class. Your 'equal' is Class B. The UFC requires Class A. Not equal."
- **vs RESERVE RIGHTS.** "Reserve rights against the UFC, see how far that gets you at the Board."

### CURE NOTICE

**What it is.** A formal written notice from the Contracting Officer advising the Contractor that specified performance failures, if not cured within 10 days (or such longer period as the CO authorizes), may result in termination for default. The Cure Notice is the nuclear threat short of actual termination. It is issued by the Contracting Officer, not by the Engineer/ROICC — but the ROICC recommends it, drafts the predicate findings, and assembles the file. Distinct from a Show Cause Notice, which is issued *after* the cure period has expired.

**Canonical references.**
- FAR 52.249-10 *Default (Fixed-Price Construction)* — the clause under which Default occurs; (a) specifies termination grounds, (b) governs excusable delays.
- FAR 49.402-3 *Procedure for Default* — procedural steps including Cure Notice and Show Cause.
- FAR 49.607 — sample Cure Notice language.
- NAVFAC P-68 / NAVFACENGCOM contracting policy — internal escalation ladder.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "This is not a letter from the ROICC. This is from the Contracting Officer. Read the signature block."
- "Ten days to cure. Day 11 is a Show Cause. Day 21 is a T4D."
- "Your QC program is a failure. That's a condition endangering performance under 52.249-10(a)(2)."
- "I've been telling you for ninety days. Now it's in writing with a clock."
- "This is your last off-ramp. Use it."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs SUBMIT RFI.** "You can't RFI your way out of a Cure Notice. The failures are documented. Cure them."
- **vs CLAIM DSC.** DSC may be an excusable-delay defense under 52.249-10(b), but only for the delay, not for the QC collapse. "DSC addresses time. It does not address the 47 open NCRs."
- **vs VALUE ENGINEER.** "You want to propose a VE while I'm holding a Cure Notice? Focus."
- **vs SCHEDULE DELAY.** A defective schedule can itself be grounds for the cure. "Failing to maintain a reasonable progress is enumerated in 52.249-10(a)(1)(ii). Your CPM is the evidence."
- **vs OR-EQUAL GAMBIT.** "Substitutions are not the cure. Performing the contract is the cure."
- **vs RESERVE RIGHTS.** "Reserve all the rights you want. Cure the default in ten days or exercise them at the Board after termination."

## CONTRACTOR MOVES

### SUBMIT RFI

**What it is.** Request for Information. A written contractor inquiry asking the Government to clarify, interpret, or resolve an ambiguity in the contract documents. In industry practice (per CMAA and Navigant studies) the average federal project sees 6–10 RFIs per million dollars of contract value, with each RFI consuming 8 hours of combined review time and costing the project roughly $1,000–$1,500 in soft costs. Strategically, the RFI is the contractor's preferred pre-claim tool: it creates paper, establishes notice, and can be weaponized to manufacture the basis for a later constructive change.

**Canonical references.**
- UFGS 01 33 00 *Submittal Procedures* — RFI format and routing (some contracts break RFIs out separately in 01 31 19 *Project Meetings* or by SpecsIntact supplement).
- FAR 52.236-21 *Specifications and Drawings for Construction* — order of precedence when documents conflict.
- FAR 52.243-4 *Changes* — how an RFI response becomes a constructive change.
- CMAA *RFI Management* guidance and the 2013 Navigant *RFI study* (industry basis for "6 RFIs/$M").
- Contract-specific RFI log maintained by the Contractor QC office.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "RFI 247. Please clarify paragraph 23 05 93 §3.2.1."
- "Without a response by close of business Thursday, the critical path will be impacted."
- "The spec and the drawings conflict. Under 52.236-21 the spec governs, but I need written direction."
- "Note: this RFI is submitted without prejudice to our rights under FAR 52.243-4 in the event the response constitutes a change."
- "Respectfully, 'refer to the spec' is not a response. The spec is the ambiguity."
- "I've got 38 open. Your office is on day 22 for RFI 212."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs REJECT SUBMITTAL.** "The submittal is not conforming because the spec is internally inconsistent — see my RFI. Resolve the RFI, then I can conform the submittal. You're rejecting a symptom."
- **vs RED-LINE SPEC.** "Your red-lines contradict paragraph 3.2. I'm RFIing the contradiction. Your markup doesn't clear the ambiguity, it doubles it."
- **vs INVOKE SHALL.** "Fine — 'shall.' Shall what, exactly? The verb's mandatory, the object's undefined. RFI is open until you tell me the target."
- **vs ISSUE NCR.** "NCR is premature. The work is built to the spec as I reasonably interpreted it; your interpretation is in dispute — see RFI 199. Pull the NCR until the RFI closes."
- **vs CITE UFC.** "The UFC wasn't listed on the reference-documents page of the section. RFI is: does the UFC govern or the commercial standard named in the spec?"
- **vs CURE NOTICE.** "The predicate failures rest on four unanswered RFIs over 60 days old. That's Government-caused delay under 52.249-10(b). Revoke the Cure Notice or we meet at the Board."

### CLAIM DSC

**What it is.** Differing Site Conditions claim under FAR 52.236-2. Two types: **Type I** — subsurface or latent physical conditions at the site differing materially from those indicated in the contract. **Type II** — unknown physical conditions at the site, of an unusual nature, differing materially from those ordinarily encountered and generally recognized as inhering in the work of the character provided for in the contract. DSC is a contractor's most-loved claim vehicle because it converts risk the contractor nominally bore at bid into a compensable equitable adjustment. Notice is required "promptly, and before the conditions are disturbed."

**Canonical references.**
- FAR 52.236-2 *Differing Site Conditions* — the clause itself, including notice requirement.
- FAR 52.236-3 *Site Investigation and Conditions Affecting the Work* — the risk the contractor accepts.
- FAR 52.243-4 *Changes* — equitable-adjustment mechanism.
- Geotechnical report referenced in the RFP (the "baseline" against which Type I is measured).
- Leading cases: *Stuyvesant Dredging*, *P.J. Maffei Building Wrecking*, *Renda Marine* — recite at your peril.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "Notice of Differing Site Conditions. Type I. The GBR shows CL-ML; we encountered weathered siltstone at elevation -6."
- "We have not disturbed the condition. Come look."
- "Equitable adjustment forthcoming under 52.243-4. This is not a claim yet — this is notice."
- "The boring logs showed three logs within 200 feet. My excavation is between them and nothing like them."
- "Type II in the alternative — rock at this elevation is not ordinarily encountered on a coastal Navy base."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs REJECT SUBMITTAL.** "The submittal was built to the drawings and the GBR baseline. You're rejecting it because the site changed, which is the very predicate of the DSC. Withdraw the rejection or process the equitable adjustment."
- **vs RED-LINE SPEC.** "Your red-lines don't change what's in the ground. The DSC is a field condition; it pre-dates your markup."
- **vs INVOKE SHALL.** "The specification said we 'shall' install pile to -25. At -18 we have refusal on rock. The mandatory verb doesn't drive the pile through granite."
- **vs ISSUE NCR.** "NCR cites insufficient embedment. Embedment is insufficient because the DSC made the specified embedment impossible. Withdraw the NCR pending modification."
- **vs CITE UFC.** "UFC 3-220-01 contemplates 'design-appropriate' soil; the geotechnical baseline is project-specific. The UFC doesn't tell me what's under this site. The baseline does, and the baseline is wrong."
- **vs CURE NOTICE.** "The progress failure is Government-caused: the DSC requires a redesign, which the Government has not issued. Excusable delay under 52.249-10(b). Revoke or we're at the Board."

### VALUE ENGINEER

**What it is.** Value Engineering Change Proposal (VECP). Contractor-initiated proposal under FAR 52.248-3 to change the contract in a way that reduces cost without impairing essential function. If accepted, the cost savings are shared per the clause (typically 55% contractor / 45% Government on fixed-price construction). Contractor's strategic use: propose the VECP late enough that the schedule impact of implementation, plus the share of savings, exceeds the value of just performing the original scope — and pocket the difference. Beloved by contractors, viewed with suspicion by engineers.

**Canonical references.**
- FAR 52.248-3 *Value Engineering — Construction* — VECP procedure, sharing ratios, acceptance authority.
- FAR 48.1 — VE policy.
- SAVE International's VE Job Plan (Function Analysis / Creative / Evaluation / Development / Presentation).
- UFC 1-200-01 and project-specific salient characteristics — the "essential function" floor a VECP cannot breach.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "VECP 003. Substitute polyurethane-coated rigid board for the specified XPS. Net life-cycle savings $847K, 55/45 split."
- "Function is preserved. R-value is preserved. Only the product class changes."
- "Under 52.248-3 the Government has 45 days to respond. We expect a decision."
- "Acceptance is at the Government's sole discretion. We acknowledge. But declining a $400K Government share without rationale is unusual."
- "If you'd prefer, we can proceed with the baseline at full cost. The offer stands."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs REJECT SUBMITTAL.** "The submittal implements the VECP that's been on your desk for 60 days. Accept the VECP and the submittal is conforming."
- **vs RED-LINE SPEC.** "Your red-lines presuppose the baseline. The VECP replaces the baseline. Red-line the VECP instead, or accept it as submitted."
- **vs INVOKE SHALL.** "The 'shall' applies to the baseline. The VECP is a Contracting Officer-accepted modification. Different verb now."
- **vs ISSUE NCR.** "Not non-conforming — executing the VECP. See Mod 0012 dated 15 January."
- **vs CITE UFC.** "The VECP is UFC-compliant. We ran the criteria check. Your UFC objection goes to the prior baseline, not the proposal."
- **vs CURE NOTICE.** "The cure is acceptance of VECP 007 and 008, which have been sitting unanswered past the 45-day window. The Government's failure to act is the root cause."

### SCHEDULE DELAY

**What it is.** Manipulation of the Critical Path Method (CPM) schedule to document, allocate, or manufacture project delay. Under UFGS 01 32 16.00 20 (NAVFAC) or 01 32 17.00 20 (design-build) the Contractor submits a baseline CPM at NTP + 60 days and updates monthly. A Time Impact Analysis (TIA) is the vehicle for a time-extension request. Contractor play: drive the critical path through an activity the Government is likely to cause delay on, then TIA every Government slip into a compensable and non-compensable time extension.

**Canonical references.**
- UFGS 01 32 16.00 20 *Project Schedule* — CPM requirements, update frequency, TIA format.
- FAR 52.242-14 *Suspension of Work* — Government-caused delay, compensable.
- FAR 52.249-10(b) *Default* — excusable (non-compensable) delay categories.
- FAR 52.243-4 *Changes* — constructive change predicate for delay claims.
- AACE International RP 29R-03 — Forensic Schedule Analysis guidance.
- Contract-specific liquidated damages clause — the stakes.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "Time Impact Analysis attached. 27 calendar days. Compensable under 52.242-14."
- "The as-built critical path runs through your submittal review cycle."
- "Concurrent delay? Respectfully, the Government's is non-concurrent — yours started 14 days later."
- "Float is a shared resource until the CPM baseline is accepted. After that, it's mine."
- "We are preserving our right to claim acceleration if directed to maintain the original completion date."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs REJECT SUBMITTAL.** "Submittal review is a Government responsibility with a contract-defined window. You're at day 31 of a 20-day review. The delay is yours."
- **vs RED-LINE SPEC.** "Your red-line adds scope that wasn't in the baseline CPM. TIA inbound for the added duration."
- **vs INVOKE SHALL.** "'Shall' doesn't move the calendar. The Contract Completion Date is set and I'm entitled to time for your interference."
- **vs ISSUE NCR.** "Rework is on the critical path only because your inspection cycle took 11 days. Partial concurrence."
- **vs CITE UFC.** "The UFC doesn't promise a completion date. The contract does. And your review of UFC-referenced criteria is why we're behind."
- **vs CURE NOTICE.** "The cure period itself is impossible inside 10 days given the schedule impact of the disputed scope. Request extension under 52.249-10(b) for Government-caused delay."

### OR-EQUAL GAMBIT

**What it is.** Proposal to substitute a product not specifically named in the spec for one that is, on the theory that the substitute is "equal" under the traditional "brand name or equal" clause. Modern NAVFAC specs use salient-characteristics tables in Section 01 60 00 *Product Requirements* to define what "equal" means; the contractor's burden is to demonstrate the substitute meets every listed characteristic. In practice contractors fish with submittals, hoping the reviewer misses a characteristic.

**Canonical references.**
- FAR 52.236-5 *Material and Workmanship* — conformity requirement.
- UFGS 01 60 00 *Product Requirements* — salient characteristics, substitution procedure.
- UFGS 01 33 00 — SD-03 Product Data, SD-08 Manufacturer's Instructions.
- FAR 11.104 (if cited in the RFP) — brand-name-or-equal policy.
- Project-specific "or equal" evaluation table in the technical spec.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "'Or equal' substitution under Section 01 60 00. Manufacturer data attached."
- "Meets or exceeds all seven salient characteristics. See matrix at tab 3."
- "The specified manufacturer is proprietary and exceeds the UFC floor. The substitute meets the floor at 40% of cost."
- "The spec named a product not available inside the NTP schedule. Substitution is the only path."
- "Reserving the right to treat refusal as a constructive change."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs REJECT SUBMITTAL.** "Rejection cites characteristics that aren't in the salient-characteristics table. You're adding requirements post-bid — that's a change, not a rejection."
- **vs RED-LINE SPEC.** "Your red-line added characteristic #8 after my submittal was in. Either accept the substitution against the original seven, or issue a formal mod for the added characteristic."
- **vs INVOKE SHALL.** "The spec 'shall' require performance. It does not 'shall' require a manufacturer's logo. Performance is met."
- **vs ISSUE NCR.** "NCR cites unapproved substitution. The substitution is under review — open submittal 117. Premature."
- **vs CITE UFC.** "The UFC sets performance. The substitute meets the UFC performance. 'Equal' is UFC-compliant."
- **vs CURE NOTICE.** "The cure for unavailable specified product is substitution. Withholding 'or equal' approval and then threatening termination for non-delivery is Government-caused."

### RESERVE RIGHTS

**What it is.** Formal written notice that the Contractor, in performing a directed action or accepting a Government decision, does not waive its ability to pursue a claim, REA (Request for Equitable Adjustment), or appeal under the Disputes clause. Used as a protective footer on correspondence, a preamble to directed work, and a standing reservation in meeting minutes. Doesn't change the immediate outcome; preserves the record for later litigation.

**Canonical references.**
- FAR 52.233-1 *Disputes* — CDA procedure, claim certification thresholds, 60-day contracting officer decision window.
- FAR 52.243-4 *Changes* — constructive change basis.
- FAR 52.242-14 *Suspension of Work* — compensable delay basis.
- Contract Disputes Act of 1978 (41 U.S.C. §§ 7101–7109) — the statutory overlay.
- ASBCA and Civilian Board of Contract Appeals — where reserved rights eventually get tested.

**Dialog hooks.**
- "Performing as directed, reserving all rights under the contract."
- "Without prejudice to our position, we will proceed."
- "This letter is not a claim. It is a preservation of notice under 52.243-4."
- "Formal REA to follow within 30 days."
- "Please place this reservation in the meeting minutes."
- "Certified claim imminent if the CO's decision is adverse."

**Rebuttals to opposing moves.**
- **vs REJECT SUBMITTAL.** "We will resubmit as directed, reserving rights to claim the rework cost as a constructive change under 52.243-4."
- **vs RED-LINE SPEC.** "Incorporating the red-line under protest. Cost and time impact to follow."
- **vs INVOKE SHALL.** "'Shall' is noted. Performing. Reserving all rights to contest the interpretation at the Board."
- **vs ISSUE NCR.** "Correcting the cited non-conformance without prejudice to our position that the work as originally installed conformed to the spec as reasonably interpreted."
- **vs CITE UFC.** "The UFC citation is noted. We disagree that it was incorporated by reference to the scope you assert, and reserve the right to litigate the incorporation question."
- **vs CURE NOTICE.** "Responding within the cure period. This response is not an admission of any predicate failure alleged therein. All rights reserved under FAR 52.233-1."

## §2-4.4 EDITORIAL WEAPONS

UFS 1-300-02 §2-4.4 *Prohibited Words and Phrases* bans a list of terms that were historically the grease that kept federal specs ambiguous and contractors solvent. The modern UFGS is editorially scrubbed of them — but every contract awarded before the applicable revision cycle is full of them, and every legacy engineer's muscle memory types them out reflexively. The prohibition is itself a rhetorical landmine. This section catalogs how each banned term shows up in dialog.

### "shall"

- **Engineer wielding it (in character).** Core of INVOKE SHALL. Defiant: "The spec says SHALL. That means mandatory. I don't care what the editorial manual says — I care what the contract says."
- **Engineer acknowledging the prohibition (also in character).** "Yes, §2-4.4 killed it. Take that up with NAVFAC HQ. The contract language we awarded still binds both of us."
- **Contractor needling.** "§2-4.4 called. It wants its mandatory verb back." / "SHALL got banned three editorial cycles ago. Catch up." / "Every time you INVOKE SHALL you're citing a clause the editorial manual disowned."
- **Engineer counter-needle.** "Modern specs say 'Install.' Do you know what that sounds like? A recipe card. I write contracts."

### "should" (in body text)

- **Engineer's view.** Only permitted in designer notes. Any "should" in the actual spec body is a red-line target. "'Should' in a binding clause is a bug, not a feature. I red-lined it to 'must' before the contract went out."
- **Contractor's exploit.** "The spec says we *should* grout the joints. Should is not shall. We elected not to." / "Advisory language. We complied with what was mandatory."
- **Engineer rebuttal.** "It's a drafting error. The RFP intent is clear." (Contractor: "Intent isn't enforceable. The words are.")

### "as shown on the drawings"

- **The trap.** Overused phrase that's unenforceable if the drawings don't, in fact, show it. The DOR inevitably missed a detail.
- **Engineer rant.** "Every time I see 'as shown on the drawings' in a spec I know the detail isn't on the drawings. It's the tell."
- **Contractor weaponization.** "The spec says install per 'as shown on the drawings.' The drawings don't show it. We installed per industry standard. §2-4.4 violation on the spec; you can't enforce what isn't drawn."
- **Engineer counter.** "'As shown' is an editorial sin. The intent is clear. Perform or I'll NCR you and we'll argue it at the Board."

### "as may be required" / "as necessary" / "as approved/directed/determined by the Contracting Officer" / "an approved type"

- **The escape clauses.** Functionally hand the CO a unilateral scope-expansion lever and are unenforceable in either direction.
- **Contractor exploit.** "'Additional bracing as necessary.' Necessary per whom? Undefined, therefore unenforceable. Installed zero additional bracing. §2-4.4." / "'An approved type' — approved by whom, measured against what? The spec is void for vagueness on this line item."
- **Engineer's authentic counter.** "I scrub those phrases out of my red-lines. When I see 'as may be required' in a submittal narrative, I flag it. I don't let the contractor have them any more than the spec writer should have."

### "first class workmanship" / "in a neat and workmanlike manner" / "securely" / "thoroughly" / "suitable" / "properly" / "good working order" / "neatly" / "carefully"

- **The workmanship sludge.** Classic feel-good spec language. All banned. All ubiquitous in legacy documents.
- **Contractor exploit.** "Installed 'in a neat and workmanlike manner.' Define 'neat.' You can't. §2-4.4 prohibits the phrase because it's unenforceable. NCR withdrawn." / "'Properly grounded' — properly per what standard? NEC? UL? NFPA 70? The spec doesn't say. Installed to NEC."
- **Engineer rant.** "'Neat and workmanlike.' That's what we used to write when we didn't know what we wanted. Now we're supposed to write 'install with 1/8-inch tolerance per edge.' You know what? The old way worked, because everyone knew what it meant. Now we argue over millimeters."
- **Engineer's professional counter.** "On my red-lines I replace 'properly' with a measurable tolerance. That's why my specs are enforceable and the DOR's aren't."

### "in this specification"

- **Problem.** Vague self-reference. §2-4.4 requires citing the specific paragraph.
- **Contractor.** "The spec says 'in this specification' — where 'in this specification'? Section? Paragraph? Undefined. Can't be cited, can't be enforced."
- **Engineer.** "I cite paragraph numbers. That's the job. 'In this specification' is lazy spec-writing."

### "Including But Not Limited To" (IBNLT)

- **Prohibition theory.** Either enumerate the list fully or use an open-ended phrase the contract supports. IBNLT creates a false-enumeration trap.
- **Contractor.** "The scope says 'Including But Not Limited To A, B, C.' We did A, B, and C. The 'not limited to' is §2-4.4-prohibited and void for vagueness. Scope complete." / "If you wanted D, you should have written D. 'IBNLT' isn't a requirement, it's a wish."
- **Engineer.** "IBNLT is lazy. I killed it in my specs years ago and replaced it with enumerated scope. But the DOR's spec here is a copy-paste festival. That's the DOR's problem — and yours."

### Herein family ("herein" / "therein" / "thereof" / "hereinafter")

- **Prohibition theory.** Antiquated cross-reference that almost always obscures what's being referenced.
- **Contractor.** "'Stored herein.' Herein where? The spec? The sentence? The section? §2-4.4 violation — cross-reference is undefined."
- **Engineer.** "The Herein family went out with carbon paper. If I see 'hereinafter referred to as the Contractor' in one more set of general conditions I'm going to write a memo. ... I already wrote the memo. Nobody reads it."

### "per" (as "in accordance with")

- **OK usage.** Unit rates like "100 Btu/hr per sf" or "3 cfm per ton."
- **Prohibited usage.** "Install per manufacturer's instructions." Should be "Install in accordance with manufacturer's instructions" — or better, the imperative mood variant.
- **Contractor.** "'Per the drawings' — §2-4.4 prohibits that usage. Under 'in accordance with,' there's ambiguity in how far the drawings control."
- **Engineer.** "'Per' is fine for rates. 'In accordance with' is for references. Anyone who writes 'per the spec' is telling on themselves."

### "/" (slash) except in unit rates

- **Prohibition.** "Approved/Directed/Determined" — all three verbs prohibited individually and the slash concatenation doubly so.
- **Dialog use.** Mostly a quiet editorial violation; shows up when engineer rants about legacy spec copy-paste ("A slash isn't a conjunction. It's indecision pretending to be precision.").

### "Contractor must provide X" (shall-substitute that's still wrong)

- **Prohibition theory.** Specs are already directed to the Contractor. Saying "Contractor must" is redundant and editorially sloppy; the imperative "Provide X" is correct.
- **Engineer.** "'Contractor must provide.' Of course the Contractor must provide — who else would? Just write 'Provide.' This is what happens when writers replace 'shall' without understanding why."
- **Contractor exploit (rare).** "The spec at 3.2.A says 'the Contractor must provide' but at 3.2.B says 'provide.' Two different voices — ambiguity on whether 3.2.B is directed to us. RFI submitted."

### Closing rhetorical notes for role-play

- **Engineer's voice.** Legacy, credentialed, specific. Cites clause numbers reflexively. Mourns editorial modernization. Red-lines the weasel words the contractor likes. Uses "shall" with defiant awareness. Writes in complete sentences, in daily reports, in pen.
- **Contractor's voice.** Younger, quicker, quantitative. Tracks RFIs and float in Primavera. Knows every escape clause in §2-4.4 by heart because each one is a free option. Cites sections without sentimentality. Fond of "without prejudice," "reserving rights," and "respectfully."
- **The central joke.** Two professionals who agree on what the words mean but disagree on whether the words are binding. The editorial manual says one thing, the contract says another, the Board will eventually say a third, and in the meantime the work has to get built.
